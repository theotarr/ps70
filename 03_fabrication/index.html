Week 3: Fabrication

For this week's fabrication assignment, I built a mechanical device to balance a marble on the edge of a cam-driven platform. The goal was to create a stable yet dynamic setup using laser-cut cardboard components for the main structure and hand-crafted elements for the motor integration and cam mechanism.

Design and Laser Cutting

Building on the 2D design skills developed in Week 2, I used Fusion to model the base, track, rails, and mounting piece for the track. These pieces were optimized for the laser cutter's material constraints—using 3mm cardboard—to ensure precise fits for assembly without additional fasteners.

The laser cutting process was straightforward: I exported the designs as .DXF files and ran them through the cutter. This produced clean edges and interlocking joints, allowing for a sturdy frame that could support the dynamic motion required for the marble balancing.

Assembly

Once cut, assembly was easy. The base and rails slotted together seamlessly, forming the core frame. I attached the track using the pre-designed mounting piece and tabs with hot glue. This method was ideal for quick prototyping due to its ease of use and physical flexibility, which was particularly helpful in accommodating the subtle motor movements without rigid constraints. At this stage, the structure was rigid and ready for the mechanical heart: the motor and cam system, which would be hand-crafted.

Iterating on Motor Mount and Cam Setup

The most challenging part was integrating the DC motor and crafting the cam to precisely control the platform's tilt, ensuring the marble teeters on the very edge without falling. Rather than relying solely on digital modeling for these irregular components, I used hand tools like scissors and a razor for quick tests.

Each iteration focused on alignment: the motor needed to be positioned so its shaft drove the cam without wobbling and pushed up the track enough that the marble wouldn't fall. After about five prototypes, I settled on a design that used a simple bracket glued to the frame, providing stable mounting.

For the cam itself, I experimented with different eccentric shapes. The key was achieving a subtle oscillation—too much tilt and the marble rolls off; too little and it doesn't balance dramatically. Through trial and error, attaching and detaching prototypes with tape, I refined the cam until the marble hovered perfectly on the edge during the peak of each rotation. This approach allowed for quick feedback loops that digital simulation couldn't match at this scale.

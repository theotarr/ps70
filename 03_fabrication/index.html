Week 3: Hand Tools & Fabrication

For this week's fabrication assignment, I built a device to balance a marble on the edge of a platform. The goal was to experiment with hand tools and fabrication using laser-cut cardboard components for the main structure and hand-crafted elements for the motor integration and cam mechanism.

Design and Laser Cutting

Building on the 2D design skills developed in Week 2, I used Fusion to model the base, track, rails, and mounting piece for the track. The laser cutting process was straightforward: I exported the designs as .DXF files and ran them through the cutter. This produced clean edges, allowing for a flexible frame that could support the rotation of the cam, pushing the track up and down.

Assembly

Once cut the pieces were easy to assemble. I glued the base and rails together, forming the core frame. I attached the track using the mounting piece and tabs with hot glue. This was ideal for quick prototyping due physical flexibility, which was particularly helpful in accommodating the subtle motor movements without rigid constraints. At this stage, the structure was ready for the the motor and cam system.

Iterating on Motor Mount and Cam Setup

The most challenging part was integrating the DC motor and crafting the cam to precisely control the platform's tilt, ensuring the marble teeters on the very edge without falling. Rather than relying solely on digital modeling for these irregular components, I used hand tools like scissors and a razor for quick tests.

Each iteration focused on alignment: the motor needed to be positioned so its shaft drove the cam without wobbling and pushed up the track enough that the marble wouldn't fall. I settled on a design that used a simple bracket glued to the frame, providing stable mounting.

For the cam itself, I experimented with different eccentric shapes. The key was achieving a subtle oscillation - too much tilt and the marble rolls off, too little and it doesn't balance dramatically. Through trial and error, attaching and detaching prototypes with tape, I refined the cam until the marble hovered perfectly on the edge during the peak of each rotation. This approach allowed for quick feedback loops that digital simulation couldn't match at this scale.

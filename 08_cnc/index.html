Week 8 — CNC: Making My Own Plates (So I Can Stop “Borrowing” from Annenberg)

The assignment this week was all about CNC machining — design something in CAD/CAM and machine it on the ShopBot. I decided to turn a small moral dilemma into a design brief: rather than “borrowing” plates and silverware from the Annenberg dining hall, I would fabricate my own. If I can make a plate I actually like, maybe I’ll stop sneaking dishes back to my dorm.

Designing the plate in Aspire

I modeled a simple oval plate directly in Aspire. The workflow felt very approachable: set the stock, sketch the oval, add a shallow pocket for the surface, and a raised rim for that classic plate feel. Aspire’s 3D preview and doing an "air cut" helped me make sure I would get what I wanted.

  • Material: plywood panel
  • Operations: pocket (interior), profile (outside)
  • Export: ShopBot toolpaths from Aspire

Cutting on the ShopBot

I clamped the sheet, zeroed the axes, and sent the Aspire toolpaths to the ShopBot. The cut took about 50 minutes.

Post‑processing the CNC mold

After the cut I cleaned up the blank so the vacuum pull would register cleanly and not catch on fuzzy edges.

  • Belt sander: Knocked off the tabs and trued the outer profile with light passes to avoid rounding the reference plane.
  • Hand sanding: Used high grit inside the tight fillet and along the rim where the orbital can’t reach; slightly eased the top edge.

A quick dust‑off with a tack cloth made a bigger difference than expected — the smoother surface produced noticeably cleaner definition in the final vacuum form.

Vacuum forming: a few tries to get it right

With the wooden mold done, I moved to the vacuum former to pull a plastic plate. This part took experimentation. My first attempts were either under‑heated (soft corners, poor definition) or a little too droopy (webbing near the rim). After tweaking heat time and putting a lot of pressure for about 30 seconds with the vacuum, I finally got a clean form that captured the rim nicely.

This project scratched two itches at once: learning the full Aspire → ShopBot → vacuum form pipeline, and easing my conscience about dishes. I now have a functional plate I made myself, and a workflow I can iterate on for different sizes or tray shapes. Next time I’d like to try a food‑safe coating on a wooden version, or thermoform a thicker sheet for durability.

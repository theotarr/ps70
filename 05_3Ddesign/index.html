Week 5 — 3D Design, Printing, and Scanning

Tesseract Design in Fusion

This week's primary challenge was to design and 3D print a tesseract, which is a three-dimensional projection of a four-dimensional hypercube. Essentially, it's a cube nested within another cube, connected by edges that create a fascinating optical illusion of depth and impossible geometry. My goal was to create a visually compelling model that highlighted this nested structure.

I used Fusion for the design, starting with a simple cube and then leveraging its robust sketching and extrusion tools to create the inner cube and the connecting framework. The process involved precise measurements and careful alignment to ensure the illusion would be effective when printed. I focused on clean lines and consistent spacing to maintain the aesthetic integrity of the design.

AirTag‑like Tracker — Enclosure Rev A

I’m designing a thin, coin‑cell powered BLE tracker enclosure sized for an nRF52‑based PCB (or, for now, a small dev board). The goals are pocket comfort, keyring attachment, and easy battery service, while keeping the shell printable without supports on common FDM printers.

Constraints and targets

  • Approx. 34–36 mm diameter (or 36×36 mm rounded square), 7.5–8.5 mm thick
  • Stackup: top shell → optional buzzer cavity → PCB (~1.6 mm) → CR2032 holder → bottom shell
  • Attachment: 4 mm slot for a split ring; generous fillets (≥1.5 mm) for comfort
  • Retention: snap‑fits with ~0.3–0.4 mm clearance; option for 2× M2 bosses
  • Serviceability: two‑piece clip with a small notch, or a bayonet battery door variant

Print settings

  • Material: PETG or ABS for toughness
  • Layer height: 0.2 mm; 3 perimeters; 20–30% gyroid infill
  • Orient to hide the seam and reinforce the keyring area along perimeters

Iteration plan

  • Print a tolerance coupon: 0.8/1.0/1.2 mm walls; 0.5–1.2 mm snap tabs
  • Fit check with CR2032 holder and dev board; adjust standoffs and clearances
  • Rev B: refine snap geometry and add optional piezo sound port

Deliverables

  • cad/airtag-body.step (master body)
  • cad/airtag-top.stl and cad/airtag-bottom.stl
  • Exploded view render: cad/exploded.png

3D Scanning: Flipper Zero

I scanned my Flipper Zero using the lab's 3D scanner. It took a few tries to get usable results, since the device's reflective screen and infrared emitter made it tricky for the scanner to pick up certain details cleanly.

To make the scanned mesh viewable in the online viewer (viewstl.com), I had to reduce the size of the STL file below 30 MiB. For that, I used the Fast Quadric Mesh Simplification tool, which lets you quickly downscale STL/OBJ models in the browser.
